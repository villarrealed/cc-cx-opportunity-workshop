Workshop purpose

Help Cisco sellers identify opportunities, find the right business stakeholders, and shape a buyable Cisco proposal pattern grounded in outcomes rather than product-first positioning.

Plain-language framing This guide uses some industry terms (CCaaS, IVR, omnichannel, AHT, NPS, deflection, Webex AI). Each is defined the first time it appears. You do not need to be a CC specialist to use this. Your job is to recognize an opportunity and pull the right Cisco people in — not to become the technical expert.

About this workshop

This is a 1-hour module inside a half-day Cisco workshop, built for Cisco sellers — AMs, AEs, SEs, and specialists — and designed to move one real account from cold context to concrete, reusable account artifacts.

What it is
  • A 1-hour module within a half-day Cisco workshop for any Cisco seller — AMs, AEs, SEs, and specialists.
  • No prior CC/CX or collaboration expertise is required — the flow is intentionally plain-language and guided.
  • An AI-assisted path that takes the seller from a cold customer name to four ready-to-use artifacts: a pre-brief, a chosen opportunity with use case, a tailored intro email + 5 discovery questions, and an outline for a customer proposal.
  • Built around a single chat thread in Cisco CIRCUIT, so each generated prompt builds on the same accumulated customer context.
What it does
  • Teaches sellers how to identify CC/CX opportunities in any account — including internal contact centers like IT help desk, HR shared services, and employee support.
  • Translates the Cisco CC/CX portfolio into plain language any seller can use: Webex Contact Center, Webex AI Agent, Webex AI Agent Assist, Webex Connect, and Webex Calling.
  • Produces concrete, account-specific artifacts in the session instead of abstract framework outputs.
  • Equips the seller to open a credible conversation with a named persona, then take the resulting proposal outline back to the AM and the CC/AI specialist for refinement.
What it is not
  • Not a deep-dive into Webex Contact Center features, CCaaS architecture, or implementation detail — that remains the specialist's job.
  • Not a replacement for the CC/AI specialist team — it is the qualification and door-opening motion that happens before specialist engagement.
  • Not a one-size-fits-all script — every artifact is tailored to the specific customer, opportunity, and persona being pursued.
  • Not a finished deliverable — the AI produces strong starting drafts; the seller and account team still validate, correct, and refine every artifact before it reaches the customer.

Match Cisco solutions to the opportunity

Now that you have a ranked list of opportunities from your AI pre-brief, the next move is to map the right Cisco capability to the opportunity you want to pursue.

Worked example: Contact Center & Customer Experience

The consultative discovery method on this page works for any Cisco solution area. From here on, the workshop walks through contact center and customer experience as the worked example — the signals, the Cisco solutions, and the prompts are all CC/CX-specific. The same flow adapts to Collaboration, Networking, Security, and other Cisco motions.

Translation up-frontWhen we say contact center (CC) or customer experience (CX), we mean anywhere a customer interacts with the business — phone calls, web chat, mobile app messages, in-branch visits, field technicians, dealer/broker/agent networks, even back-office service teams. It is not just the room with headsets and dashboards. And remember: internal service organizations (IT help desk, HR shared services, employee support) count too — often bigger than the external ones.

You do not need to be the technical expert — you just need to recognize which Cisco capability fits where, and pull in the right specialist when it is time to go deeper.

Signals to watch for
  • CCaaS / UCaaS renewal coming up, or a failing IVR that people complain about.
  • C-suite AI mandate, agent attrition, or productivity pressure.
  • Voice, chat, and digital channels are fragmented across siloed teams or vendors.
  • M&A consolidation or a new WhatsApp / RCS / digital-service push.
Buying windows
  • Incumbent contract renewal or fiscal-year planning cycle.
  • Post-M&A platform consolidation or operating-model redesign.
  • Regulatory deadline around call recording, privacy, or service controls.
  • Service incident, executive attention, or AI-transformation budget release.
External examples
  • Retail customer service and order-support operations.
  • Insurance claims, policy servicing, and banking call-center journeys.
  • Telco or utilities support where service quality and deflection matter.
Internal examples
  • IT help desk / end-user services for employees.
  • HR shared services, employee benefits, or payroll support.
  • Internal facilities, field-service, or employee support desks.

Webex Contact Center

What it is in plain terms

The cloud-delivered system that runs the customer’s contact center — handles inbound calls, routes them to the right agent or queue, integrates with CRM systems, gives supervisors dashboards, and reports on what happened. It replaces or modernizes whatever they are using today.

Examples of where it fits
  • Move off an aging on-prem contact center.
  • Consolidate multiple regional CC vendors.
  • Stand up a real internal help desk platform instead of a phone tree.
  • Compete into a CCaaS renewal or platform refresh.
When to lead with it
Lead with Webex Contact Center anywhere there is a real contact center buying decision in the next 12–18 months: vendor renewal, M&A consolidation, or platform refresh.

Webex AI

What it is in plain terms

Cisco’s AI portfolio for the contact center. It helps customers automate simple work, support agents in real time, and coach the operation using AI-driven insights.

  • AI Agent — AI handles the conversation directly for voice or chat self-service.
  • AI Agent Assist — AI sits next to the human agent, suggesting answers and summarizing calls.
  • AI-powered analytics & coaching — AI scores interactions, surfaces themes, and identifies coaching moments.
Examples of where it fits
  • Deflection pressure: too many calls, not enough agents.
  • High agent attrition and slow ramp for new hires.
  • Need to extend service hours without adding headcount.
  • Executive AI mandate with no clear starting point.
When to lead with it
Lead with Webex AI when the customer says “AI” first, or when deflection, AHT, agent productivity, or first-call resolution is explicitly in scope.

Webex Connect

What it is in plain terms

The platform that lets the customer engage on digital channels — SMS, WhatsApp, RCS, email, push, web chat, mobile in-app — and orchestrate journeys across them. It supports proactive outbound and digital-to-human escalation without bolting together point tools.

Examples of where it fits
  • Launch WhatsApp, SMS, or RCS for service.
  • Consolidate multiple vendors for SMS, chat, and email.
  • Run proactive reminders, alerts, and updates.
  • Deflect from voice into digital journeys.
When to lead with it
Lead with Webex Connect when the conversation is about digital channels, omnichannel journeys, or proactive customer communication rather than just inbound voice.

Webex Calling

What it is in plain terms

The cloud voice platform that powers every business conversation — for contact center agents, back-office experts, and the rest of the workforce. It replaces aging on-prem PBXs and fragmented regional calling vendors with one cloud calling service that natively connects to Webex Contact Center and the rest of the platform.

Examples of where it fits
  • Retire an aging on-prem PBX or end-of-life calling system.
  • Consolidate regional calling vendors after M&A.
  • Unify business calling and contact center on one platform and one bill.
  • Bring back-office experts into the conversation alongside contact center agents.
When to lead with it
Lead with Webex Calling when the customer has a calling-platform decision in flight — PBX retirement, post-M&A consolidation, or a desire to converge business calling and contact center under one vendor.
The One Cisco story

These pieces are designed to work together as one platform. Here is the story in plain language — the same way you would tell it to a customer:

  1. 1

    A customer reaches out — by phone, chat, email, or messaging app. Webex Contact Center is the system that receives the contact and connects it to the right place.

  2. 2

    Webex AI Agent answers the simple, repetitive questions on its own — checking an order, resetting a password, booking an appointment — so customers get instant answers and human agents are freed up for the harder conversations.

  3. 3

    When a human agent is needed, Webex AI Agent Assist works alongside them in real time — suggesting answers, summarizing the conversation, and surfacing the next best step so every agent sounds like an expert.

  4. 4

    Webex Connect handles the proactive side of the conversation — appointment reminders, delivery updates, two-way messaging on SMS or WhatsApp — and seamlessly hands the customer over to a live agent if the conversation needs to escalate.

  5. 5

    Underneath it all, Webex Calling is the cloud voice platform that powers every conversation — for contact center agents, back-office experts, and the rest of the workforce. One vendor, one voice platform, one bill — instead of stitching together a contact center from one provider and business calling from another.

  6. 6

    Because it is one platform, the conversation, context, and history flow together across these pieces — so the business gets a more connected view, can see what is working, fix what is not, and keep improving the experience over time.

Why this matters: Cisco delivers all of it as a single-vendor, natively built platform — contact center, AI for self-service, AI for agents, proactive digital messaging, and the underlying cloud calling platform. Customers stop stitching together five vendors and managing the integrations themselves, and start running one connected platform with one roadmap.

The 5-Question Discovery Scan

Use these in any meeting — even if the meeting is not framed as a CC discussion. They are short, plain-language, and designed for non-specialists.

When to use which

Use the static 5 questions in your first conversation when you know little about the account. Use the persona-tailored generator below once you know who you are meeting and already have customer context loaded into the same CIRCUIT thread. The static questions surface the opportunity; the tailored questions advance it.

1

“When your customers — or your own employees — need help, where do they go and how do they reach you?”

What it surfaces

Every place the business answers questions or solves problems — the customer call center, the website chat, the IT help desk, HR support, the in-store team. Many companies have several of these and don't realize they all face the same challenges.

2

“What does that experience feel like today — for the person asking, and for the people answering?”

What it surfaces

Whether the experience is consistent or fragmented. Do customers have to repeat themselves? Do employees jump between five tools to answer one question? This is where the case for one connected platform starts to land.

3

“What are you measured on, and what are you being asked to improve this year?”

What it surfaces

The business outcome the customer cares about — happier customers, faster answers, lower cost, fewer complaints, more loyalty. This becomes the anchor of the proposal. If they cannot name a measurable target, the opportunity is not real yet.

4

“Where are you using — or thinking about using — AI to help people get answers faster?”

What it surfaces

Whether AI is on their roadmap, who is pushing for it, and whether they are looking at it for customer-facing service, internal employee support, or both. Almost every executive has an AI mandate this year — this question makes it specific.

5

“What is blocking you from improving this today — and is there a contract, project, or deadline that is forcing your hand?”

What it surfaces

The two things that turn an opportunity into a deal: the wedge (what is in the way today — vendor, cost, integration, or capability gap) and the timing (a contract renewal, a board mandate, a regulatory date, or a budget cycle). If both show up, you have a real opportunity now.

How to read answers — strong signal
  • The customer names a specific business or service problem.
  • They mention a metric, target, or measurable pain.
  • They reference vendor/platform pain or ask what Cisco would do.
How to read answers — weak or non-answer
  • Weak: “we're looking at options,” “everything is fine,” or no metrics show up.
  • Non-answer: they deflect to IT, procurement, or say they have no view.
  • That is usually a persona-targeting problem, not a qualification failure.
What to do with a non-answer: if your contact cannot answer 3 of the 5, you are probably talking to the wrong person. Use the persona generator below to identify who you should be meeting and ask the AM for that intro.

What to leave the workshop with

End the session with four concrete artifacts in your chat thread — not a vague sense that CC is “interesting.” Each artifact builds on the previous one, all anchored to the same customer and opportunity.

One AI pre-brief

A completed Step 0b customer pre-brief with the top 2 opportunities ranked, financial impact ranges, and Cisco TCV estimates. Pasted into your CIRCUIT chat thread.

One chosen opportunity + use case

The Section 1 use case you decided to pursue, with quantified outcome, Cisco solution mapping, and at least one industry reference. This is your anchor for everything downstream.

A proposal outline anchored to the chosen opportunity, in the format that fits the customer's buying motion. A starting draft for the account team and CC/AI specialist to refine before it reaches the customer.
